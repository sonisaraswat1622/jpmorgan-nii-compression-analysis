The Problem

How cheap deposits became JPMorgan's most expensive problem

When the Federal Reserve raised rates from near-zero to 5.5% between 2022 and 2023 — the fastest hiking cycle in 40 years — JPMorgan initially benefited massively. Higher rates meant more income from loans and securities. NII climbed from $13B to $23B quarterly.

But depositors woke up. Customers moved money from near-zero checking accounts into high-yield savings, CDs, and money market funds. JPMorgan had to match those rates or lose billions in deposits. The funding cost surged. The gap between what they earned on assets and paid on deposits — the Net Interest Margin — started compressing.

Then the Fed started cutting rates in late 2024. Loan yields fell immediately. But deposit costs stayed elevated because millions of customers had already locked into high-rate CDs. JPMorgan was caught paying high rates while earning less. This is the deposit margin compression trap.

Chart 1 — The Full Journey

Four distinct phases — hike benefit, peak, compression, recovery

Source: JPMorgan Chase SEC 8-K filings Q1 2022 – Q4 2025. NII excluding Markets (non-GAAP) — removes trading volatility to show core lending/deposit performance.

The chart shows four clear phases: Phase 1 (2022) — rapid NII growth as rate hikes flow through with low deposit beta. Phase 2 (2023) — NII peaks at $23B quarterly as both loan yields and deposit costs peak. Phase 3 (2024) — compression bites as deposit repricing catches up and the Fed starts cutting. Phase 4 (2025) — recovery, driven not by rates but by JPMorgan's own countermeasures.

Chart 2 — Root Cause Waterfall

Exactly what drove NII from Q4 2023 peak to Q4 2025 recovery

Source: JPMorgan 8-K earnings narratives Q1 2024 – Q4 2025. Factor decomposition based on management commentary attributing NII movements by driver each quarter.

The waterfall makes the story quantifiable. Three headwinds — lower Fed rates (−$1.2B), deposit mix shift (−$0.9B), and lower CCB deposit balances (−$0.6B) — knocked a combined $2.7B off NII from the peak. JPMorgan fought back with card revolving balance growth (+$1.4B), securities reinvestment activity (+$0.7B), and wholesale deposit growth (+$0.8B not shown). The result: a net recovery to $23.5B — slightly above the Q4 2023 peak.

Chart 3 — JPMorgan's Response

Four deliberate countermeasures deployed through 2025

Source: JPMorgan 8-K management commentary Q1–Q4 2025 and Investor Day 2025 presentation. Impact estimates derived from management attribution language per quarter.
What JPMorgan actually did
  • Card revolving balance growth: Grew high-yield credit card revolvers from $228B to $261B (+14.5% YoY) — these earn ~20%+ APR, far above deposit costs
  • Wholesale deposit growth: Aggressively grew institutional deposits in Payments and Securities Services (+16% YoY in Q3 2025) — cheaper than retail CDs
  • Securities reinvestment: Reinvested maturing securities at higher prevailing rates, improving the fixed-income yield on the balance sheet
  • Balance sheet expansion: Grew total loans 11% YoY in 2025 — more earning assets to offset compressed margin on existing ones
What still isn't solved
  • Deposit compression persists: Every 2025 earnings release still lists "deposit margin compression" as a headwind — it hasn't gone away
  • 2026 guidance acknowledges it: JPM's $104.5B NII target explicitly assumes compression as a $2B+ headwind offset by volume growth
  • Rate cuts still pressure margins: Two more Fed cuts assumed in 2026 — each cut reduces asset yields faster than deposit costs fall
  • Consumer deposit mix still low: Non-interest bearing deposits at 24% of total — less than half the 53% seen in 2021
Chart 4 — NII Guidance vs Reality

$104.5B target for 2026 — ambitious but achievable

Source: JPMorgan annual reports, earnings releases, and Q4 2025 investor presentation (January 13, 2026). 2026 is JPMorgan's central case guidance as of Jan 2026.

JPMorgan's 2026 NII target of $104.5B is a significant step up — but it's not pure optimism. The path assumes: $4.5B from balance sheet growth and mix improvement, partially offset by $2B from lower rates and continued deposit margin compression. The remaining lift comes from Markets NII (~$9.5B vs $3.3B in 2025), which is more variable.

The key risk: if the Fed cuts more than twice, or if deposit competition re-intensifies (as it did in 2023), the volume-growth story may not fully offset the margin headwind. This is the ongoing tension a BA should be tracking.

Chart 5 — Deposit Mix Normalisation

The yield-seeking migration is slowing — but not reversing

Source: JPMorgan Investor Day 2025 presentation slide on NIB deposit % (economic and reported views). Q4 2021 to Q1 2025.

The rate of migration from cheap non-interest bearing deposits into expensive CDs and money market accounts is slowing. JPMorgan's own Investor Day 2025 presentation showed the NIB share decline stabilising around 24% — down from 53% in 2021 but no longer falling sharply. Management described this as "normalization of yield-seeking behavior" — customers who wanted high-rate products have largely already moved.

This is actually the most positive structural signal in the data. Once the migration stabilises, deposit costs stop rising — and any future rate cuts become a tailwind rather than a partial headwind.

Chart 6 — New NII Engines

Card revolvers and loan growth replacing rate-driven NII

Source: JPMorgan 8-K quarterly earnings releases Q1 2024 – Q3 2025. Average revolving card balances and average total loan portfolio.

JPMorgan's recovery isn't just waiting for rates to normalise — it's actively building new NII engines. Credit card revolving balances grew steadily through 2024–25, contributing meaningfully to NII at high effective yields. Total loans grew 11% YoY in 2025 — driven by wholesale lending, auto, and Card Services. This is the structural shift: JPMorgan is generating more NII at lower margins, through higher volume.

Timeline

The full story from crisis to recovery

2022 — Rate Hike Benefit
Fed raises rates from 0.25% to 4.0%. JPMorgan's NII nearly doubles from $13B to $19B quarterly. Low deposit beta = banks keep the benefit. Stock rallies.
Q4 2023 — Peak NII
NII ex-Markets hits $23.0B quarterly. But deposit costs are catching up fast — customers have figured out high-yield savings exist.
Sept 2024 — The Guidance Cut
JPM President Daniel Pinto tells analysts the 2025 NII consensus is "not very reasonable." Stock drops 7% in one session. Deposit margin compression is officially a crisis.
2024–2025 — Countermeasures Deployed
Card revolve growth accelerated. Wholesale deposit strategy launched. Securities reinvestment programme initiated. Total loan portfolio expanded 11% YoY. NII finds a floor.
Q4 2025 — New Post-Crisis High
NII ex-Markets hits $23.5B — above the Q4 2023 peak. FY 2025 NII: $95.9B (+3% YoY). Deposit mix migration slowing. JPMorgan targets $104.5B for 2026.
2026 — Still a Headwind
Deposit margin compression explicitly listed as a $2B+ headwind in 2026 guidance. Two more Fed rate cuts assumed. The problem is managed — not solved.
